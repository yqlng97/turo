## What Actually Is Turo?

Turo is a peer-to-peer car sharing marketplace. Think Airbnb, but instead of renting someone's spare bedroom, you're renting someone's actual car. Private car owners â€” called "hosts" â€” list their vehicles on the platform, set their own prices, and regular people â€” "guests" â€” book them directly through Turo's app or website.

It launched back in 2010 (originally called RelayRides) out of Boston, and it's grown into the world's largest car sharing marketplace. As of late 2024, Turo reported 150,000 active hosts globally, 350,000 vehicle listings, and 3.5 million active guests. The platform operates across the United States, Canada, the United Kingdom, Australia, and France.

The model is pretty simple: you search for a car near your destination, pick one you actually like, book it, and either pick it up from the host or sometimes have it delivered to you. No counter. No paperwork shuffling. No guessing what car you'll end up with.

## Guest Protection Plans: What They Cost and What They Cover

This is the part people always ask about. Turo isn't a rental company in the traditional sense, so insurance works a little differently. Every trip comes with liability coverage, but you also choose a protection plan that determines how much you'd be on the hook for if the car gets damaged.

All guest plans provide the same $750,000 liability coverage through Travelers but vary in the out-of-pocket expenses a guest is responsible for if the car is damaged.

| Protection Plan | Cost | Your Max Out-of-Pocket (Physical Damage) | Liability Coverage |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Premier** | 65â€“100% of trip price (min. $14/day) | $0 | Up to $750K |
| **Standard** | 40% of trip price (min. $12/day) | $500 | Up to $750K |
| **Minimum** | ~10% of trip price (min. $10/day) | $3,000 | Up to $750K |
| **Decline** | $0 | Full responsibility | State minimum only |

The Premier plan offers the highest level of protection â€” the primary guest pays no out-of-pocket costs for reported physical damage, excluding eligible mechanical or interior damage. The Standard plan limits the guest's out-of-pocket responsibility for physical damage to $500, and a $500 damage deposit is collected upfront and refunded if damages fall below that amount or are ineligible.

One thing worth knowing: most travel credit cards do not offer coverage for ride-sharing companies like Turo, so it's worth checking with your card issuer before you skip the protection plan entirely.
